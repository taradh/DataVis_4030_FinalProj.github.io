Diversity in Big Tech

How Apple, Google, and Facebook "stand" in solidarity but lack in diversity

November 19, 2020 by Colton Smith, Tarad Humphrey & Jada Houser

The Diversity Conversation Today

In the aftermath of the murders of Breonna Taylor, Ahmaud Arbery, George Floyd, and countless others, the United States has been forced to reckon with the long history of prejudice and discrimination which is tightly woven into its institutions. Many companies have issued statements speaking out against racism, “standing” in solidarity with the larger #BlackLivesMatter movement urging for justice. Even big tech, as documented by The Plug, spoke out, including the likes of Apple, Google, and Facebook.

In tandem with larger conversations about race in the United States, there has been a resurgence in the discussion of diversity and inclusion and its benefits in the workplace. In a 2020 report by management consultancy, McKinsey & Company, among 1,000 public companies across North and Latin America and the United Kingdom, those companies who had greater ethnic and cultural diversity within their executive leadership saw 36 percent more in profits than those without such diversity. In a report conducted by Forbes, they find, through surveys and interviews of 321 diversity and inclusion executives across the globe, that many recognize that diversity is "crucial to encouraging different perspectives and ideas that foster innovation”. The Harvard Business Review reported that companies who have diverse talent are 70 percent more likely to tap into a new market, as “inherently diverse contributors understand the unmet needs of under-leveraged markets”.

The Numbers

As big tech has largely assisted with the documentation of police brutality and social justice movements, through viral iPhone footage, trending Twitter hashtags, and top Google searches, we seek to investigate the extent to which big tech’s solidarity statements are reflective of their workforce. We examine Apple, Google, and Facebook’s diversity reports, recording the makeup of their workforce by gender and race from 2014 to 2018.

Actions—Past, Present and Future—Speak Louder Than Words

Given the current cultural climate and data endorsing the benefits of diversity in the workplace, the time is now for big tech, and other companies, to examine their current standing, recognize their shortfalls and develop real, effective solutions to the lack of representation within their companies.
